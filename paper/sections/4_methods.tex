\section{Methods and Data}\label{empirical-methodology}

We investigate whether the empirically observed behaviour of different closeness formulations matches the theoretical prediction. The purpose is diagnostic: we use correlations with urban variables to detect whether formulations behave differently, not to model centrality--land-use relationships or make causal claims. If formulations measured the same underlying property, they would exhibit similar correlation patterns; divergent correlations constitute evidence of divergent mathematical behaviour. Network centralities are associated with land-use intensities \cite{Porta2009} and pedestrian volumes \cite{Penn1998, sharmin_meta-analysis_2018}: we therefore expect positive correlations for formulations that scale consistently regardless of varying subgraph sizes. The theoretical hypothesis in Section~\ref{closeness_centrality} predicts \emph{a priori} which formulations should exhibit positive, negative, or negligible associations.

\subsection{Data}

The analysis uses Madrid, Spain. The street network is a high-quality cleaned road-centreline dataset from CNIG/IGN \cite{madrid_street_network}. Land uses are derived from \n{153953} geocoded premises classified across approximately 80 categories \cite{madrid_premises}. Origin-destination travel data is drawn from the Regional Transport Consortium of Madrid travel survey (${\sim}\n{223000}$ journeys, ${\sim}\n{1260}$ zones) \cite{crtm_edm_2020, crtm_zones_2020}. Madrid was selected for the availability of high-quality network data and high-resolution land-use and trip information. Full data preparation notes, code, and datasets are provided in open repositories.

\subsection{Centrality Measures}

Centralities are computed using localised analysis from $500m$ to $10km$, capturing scales from local walkability to regional accessibility. All measures are computed on a dual representation of road-centreline street segments (not an axial or topological representation) using both \emph{metric} and \emph{geometric} (angular) distances. The following closeness variants are denoted as follows in plots:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Closeness}: standard non-normalised formulation.
  \item \emph{Closeness $N^{1}$}: \emph{Normalised Closeness}, commonly cited in the literature.
  \item \emph{Closeness $N^{1.2}$}: NAIN ($N^{1.2}/\sum_{j}d_{(i,j)}$), an empirical radius-alignment correction derived in \citet{Hillier2012}.
  \item \emph{Improved Closeness} ($N^{2}$): the variant which divides node count by average rather than total distance.
\end{enumerate}
\emph{Harmonic Closeness}, \emph{Gravity Index}, \emph{Betweenness}, and length-weighted variants are also computed; details and continuous length-weighted forms are described in the Supplementary Materials.

\subsection{Correlation Approach}

Land-use accessibility is calculated using a gravity-style formulation (Equation~\ref{eq:gravity}) weighting nearby opportunities more heavily than distant ones \cite{Sevtsuk2012, Iacono2008}. For the inferential analysis we use a PCA-based land-use intensity index formed across five categories and five distances; the first component captures $\pcaVarianceOne$ of variance (loadings in Supplementary Materials; Figure~\ref{fig:pca_loadings}) and is the target for the block-bootstrap confidence intervals. We additionally report stand-alone Food \& Beverage and Retail accessibility at 200m (mean walk distance ${\approx}70m$) as directly interpretable cross-checks.

For a street segment (dual node) $i$, accessibility is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gravity}
  A_{(i)}=\sum_{j}\exp(-\beta\cdot d_{(i,j)})
\end{equation}
where $d_{(i,j)}$ is the shortest-path network distance and $\beta$ controls decay rate, parameterised via distance thresholds (see Supplementary Materials).

Trip data comprises records filtered to non-home destinations (purposes 2--6), aggregated to origin-destination zones and correlated with average segment centralities within each zone ($\nZonesFinal$ zones after filtering). All correlations use Spearman's rank coefficient $\rho$, appropriate for skewed distributions without requiring transformation. Correlations tend to be stronger at greater thresholds due to aggregation smoothing \cite{Fotheringham1991} and are most directly compared between measures at the same scale. Descriptive statistics for land uses and zone-level outcomes are in the Supplementary Materials; full centrality descriptive statistics are provided in the code repository.

\subsection{Spatial Autocorrelation}\label{sec:spatial_autocorr}

Closeness measures exhibit positive spatial autocorrelation. We quantify this using Moran's $I$ \cite{moran_notes_1950} with $k$-nearest neighbour weights (where $k$ is set to the median number of neighbouring segments at each threshold), report effective sample sizes via $N_{\text{eff}} \approx N(1-I)/(1+I)$ \cite{Clifford1989}, and construct 95\% confidence intervals using a spatial block bootstrap (100 spatial clusters, 1,000 resamples). Given that this study is comparative rather than causal, the comparative patterns across formulations remain valid; individual $p$-values should be interpreted with caution. The $N_{\text{eff}}$ adjustment is a first-order approximation \cite{Clifford1989}, reported only as an indication of the autocorrelation penalty; Moran's $I$ is computed at the thresholds (\distm{500}--\distm{2000}) where the $k$-nearest-neighbour weights are tractable, where it is robust to the choice of $k$ (a $k\in\{8,20,50\}$ sweep gives consistent values). The block bootstrap does not rely on these weights (it resamples whole spatial clusters) so its percentile confidence intervals span the full localised range (\distm{500}--\distkm{10}). Full results are in the Supplementary Materials (Tables~\ref{tab:morans_i}--\ref{tab:bootstrap_ci}).
