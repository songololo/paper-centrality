\section{Introduction}

Streets form connections between diverse activities and places. While their functional role is apparent, their emergent properties can be less so. We routinely observe that particular sidewalks are livelier than others, that certain land uses cluster at particular locations, or that walkable and vibrant streets can become public destinations in their own right \cite{Jacobs1961}. Yet, without recourse to the incremental and evolutionary development typical of historical towns and cities, it is not straightforward to anticipate these dynamics for planned street networks \cite{Alexander1967}. Even if the benefits of compact and walkable forms of urban development are widely reported, new developments continue to prioritise vehicular movement to more distant places at the expense of pedestrians and connectivity to local street networks \cite{transport_for_new_homes_what_2025}. This is where network analytic methods hold particular relevance: by enabling the comparison of emergent properties of street configurations, they make it feasible to explore how these relate to potential activity and use.

Network analysis has been widely utilised across numerous disciplines, from early centrality methods in the study of telecommunication networks \cite{Shimbel1953} to more recent advances in social network analysis \cite{Wasserman1994}. The utilisation of graph theories for spatial problems can be traced to 1736 when Euler proposed a graph-based solution to the Königsberg Seven Bridges problem \cite{euler_solutio_1741}. In the 1950s and 1960s, Froshaug compared hierarchies of street networks by mapping them as simplified graphs with street intersections as nodes \cite{froshaug_visuelle_1959}. Space Syntax theory, developed by Hillier and colleagues in the late 1970s and early 1980s, integrated network measures with a social theory of space to analyse cities \cite{Hillier1984}, prompting wider adoption of street network analysis in urban design and planning.

Closeness is now widely applied through a range of packages such as \texttt{depthmapX} \cite{Hillier2012}, \texttt{Place Syntax Tool} \cite{stahle_place_2023}, \texttt{cityseer} \cite{simons_cityseer_2023}, \texttt{sDNA} \cite{cooper_sdna_2020}, and the \texttt{Urban Network Analysis} toolbox \cite{Sevtsuk2012}; as well as general-purpose libraries such as \texttt{igraph} \cite{csardi_igraph_2006}, \texttt{tidygraph}/\texttt{sfnetworks} \cite{pedersen_tidygraph_2024, vanderbie_sfnetworks_2021}, and \texttt{NetworkX} \cite{hagberg_networkx_2008}.
These tools commonly operate with distance-bounded catchments to mitigate edge effects, enable cross-city comparisons, and permit multi-scalar investigation of network properties \cite{Turner2007, van_nes_introduction_2021}. Yet while localised analysis addresses these practical concerns, it also changes how closeness formulations behave. Distance thresholds at each origin produce subgraphs of varying size, and certain formulations scale differently across them. This consequence is not well documented: in practice, the closeness formulation used---and its behaviour under varying subgraph sizes---is frequently left implicit, and papers continue to cite formulations that differ from those actually computed. A researcher implementing the cited formula to replicate a published study may therefore obtain different results, potentially with reversed sign or negligible magnitude.

The issue sits within a broader context of reproducibility challenges in street network analysis, including differences in network representation \cite{feng_accessibility_2022, Marshall2018}, algorithmic variations \cite{krenz_kimon_developments_2022}, and inadequate control for edge effects \cite{Gil2017}. The divergence between cited and implemented closeness formulations represents a specific, tractable instance of these challenges, one with clear mathematical explanation and practical resolution.

\paragraph{What this paper adds:} We document a divergence between the closeness formulations cited in the street network analysis literature and the variants implemented in computational packages, explain theoretically why these formulations behave differently under localised analysis, and provide an empirical demonstration with an openly reproducible workflow.

\paragraph{Research questions:} \textbf{RQ1:} Do different closeness formulations behave differently under localised street network analysis? \textbf{RQ2:} Are these mathematical differences empirically detectable when centrality measures are correlated with urban indicators? We hypothesise that \emph{Closeness} will exhibit sign reversal under localised analysis because it conflates subgraph size with average distance, and that \emph{Normalised Closeness} will not correct this. By contrast, \emph{Improved} and \emph{Harmonic Closeness} should yield values that increase with both the number and proximity of reachable nodes. Section~\ref{closeness_centrality} develops this hypothesis formally; Sections~\ref{empirical-methodology}--\ref{data-analysis} demonstrate these differences empirically.
