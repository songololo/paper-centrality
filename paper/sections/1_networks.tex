\section{Introduction}

Streets form connections between diverse activities and places. While their functional role is apparent, their emergent properties can be less so. We routinely observe that particular sidewalks are livelier than others, that certain land uses cluster at particular locations, or that walkable and vibrant streets can become public destinations in their own right \cite{Jacobs1961}. Yet, without recourse to the incremental and evolutionary development typical of historical towns and cities, it is not straightforward to anticipate these dynamics for planned street networks \cite{Alexander1967}. Even if the benefits of compact and walkable forms of urban development are widely reported, new developments continue to prioritise vehicular movement at the expense of pedestrians and local connectivity \cite{transport_for_new_homes_what_2025}. This is where network analytic methods hold particular relevance: by comparing emergent properties of street configurations, it becomes feasible to explore how these relate to potential activity and use.

Network centrality methods quantify how connected or `relatively central' a node is within a network structure. Two common measures are \emph{closeness} \cite{Sabidussi1966}, reflecting proximity to other nodes, and \emph{betweenness} \cite{Freeman1977}, reflecting how often a node lies on shortest paths. Street network centrality methods are now implemented in domain-specific packages such as \texttt{depthmapX} \cite{Hillier2012}, \texttt{Place Syntax Tool} \cite{stahle_place_2023}, \texttt{cityseer} \cite{simons_cityseer_2023}, \texttt{sDNA} \cite{cooper_sdna_2020}, and the \texttt{Urban Network Analysis} toolbox \cite{Sevtsuk2012}; as well as general-purpose libraries such as \texttt{igraph} \cite{csardi_igraph_2006}, \texttt{tidygraph}/\texttt{sfnetworks} \cite{pedersen_tidygraph_2024, vanderbie_sfnetworks_2021}, and \texttt{NetworkX} \cite{hagberg_networkx_2008}.

These tools commonly operate with distance-bounded catchments (`localised' analysis) rather than computing centrality over entire networks (`global' analysis). In global analysis, centrality magnitudes are coupled to network size, boundaries are difficult to define consistently, and centrality values diminish near them due to edge effects \cite{Turner2007, van_nes_introduction_2021}. Localised analysis addresses this through a moving window (Figure~\ref{fig:moving_window}): each node is visited in turn; all nodes within a specified distance threshold are isolated as a subgraph; centrality is computed for that subgraph; the algorithm then proceeds to the next node. Measures at a given threshold can be compared across locations---even across cities---while different thresholds reveal different scales of network structure \cite{Turner2007}. Localised analysis is now standard practice.

However, distance thresholds produce subgraphs of varying size, which changes how closeness formulations scale. This consequence is not well documented, and in practice the closeness formulation used is frequently left implicit; papers commonly cite formulations that differ from those actually computed. A researcher implementing the cited formula to replicate a published study may therefore obtain different results.

This issue sits within a broader context of reproducibility challenges in street network analysis, including differences in network representation \cite{feng_accessibility_2022, Marshall2018}, algorithmic variations \cite{krenz_kimon_developments_2022}, and inadequate control for edge effects \cite{Gil2017}. The divergence between cited and implemented closeness formulations represents a specific, tractable instance of these challenges, one with clear mathematical explanation and practical resolution.

\paragraph{What this paper adds:} We document a divergence between the closeness formulations cited in the street network analysis literature and the variants implemented in computational packages, explain why these formulations behave differently under localised analysis, and replicate this behaviour on a real-world street network with an openly reproducible workflow.

\paragraph{Research questions:} \textbf{RQ1:} Do different closeness formulations behave differently under localised street network analysis? \textbf{RQ2:} Are these mathematical differences empirically detectable when centrality measures are correlated with urban indicators? The following section develops the hypothesis formally; Sections~\ref{empirical-methodology}--\ref{data-analysis} demonstrate these differences empirically.
