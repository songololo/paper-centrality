\section{Discussion and Conclusions}\label{discussion}

The empirical results for Madrid are consistent with the theoretical hypothesis. Regarding \textbf{RQ1}, mathematical \emph{Closeness} exhibits sign reversal under localised analysis, showing negative associations where positive ones are anticipated. \emph{Normalised Closeness} does not resolve this---it yields negligible associations. \emph{Improved Closeness} and \emph{Harmonic Closeness} yield values that increase with subgraph size and proximity. Regarding \textbf{RQ2}, these differences are detectable empirically: the same pattern appears for stand-alone accessibility measures, a PCA-based summary index, and origin-destination trip counts, across weighted and unweighted variants and both metric and geometric distances.

A notable intermediate case is the NAIN formulation ($N^{1.2}$) \cite{Hillier2012}: it yields stronger associations than \emph{Normalised Closeness} but does not fully recover the behaviour of \emph{Improved Closeness}, having been derived to align values across distance radii rather than to address subgraph scaling.

The reproducibility problem is most acute for researchers who implement closeness from a cited formula without access to original code, or who teach network centrality from commonly cited literature. We suspect the misattribution persists largely unnoticed because most domain-specific packages already implement variants that account for varying subgraph sizes---\emph{Improved} or \emph{Harmonic Closeness}---so outputs appear plausible to users who hold mistaken impressions of the underlying formulation. The problem surfaces when replication is attempted: a researcher implementing the cited formula independently of the original code will obtain different results \cite{turner_depthmapx_2020, stahle_place_2023, simons_cityseer_2023, hagberg_exploring_2008, csardi_igraph_2006}. Earlier studies citing \emph{Normalised Closeness} without open code, using small geographic areas, or lacking edge-effect controls \cite{Turner2007, Porta2006} may therefore merit re-examination, though some may have operated under conditions where the distinction matters less.

\subsection{Practical Guidance}

For localised street network analysis, we suggest:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Use closeness formulations that account for varying subgraph sizes, such as \emph{Improved Closeness}, \emph{Harmonic Closeness}, or \emph{Gravity}-based measures;
  \item Document the precise formulation used, not just the generic term `closeness';
  \item Provide code or specify the software package and version to enable replication;
  \item When using generic network packages, verify how the closeness implementation handles varying subgraph sizes;
  \item Use openly available reference datasets and reproducible workflows so that different measures can be benchmarked collaboratively.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

The empirical analysis draws on a single city. Because the aim is to show that mathematical divergence between closeness formulations is detectable in practice, rather than to estimate precise associations or to advocate for a particular formulation, we argue that a single case study is adequate. Robust modelling of centrality--land-use relationships, or evaluation of which formulation best predicts a given outcome, would benefit from multi-city comparisons.

The strength of association between centrality measures and urban variables will differ across cities and correlates, for at least two reasons. City morphology is one source of variation: Madrid's relatively diffuse, polycentric land-use pattern suits closeness-like measures, whereas cities organised around pronounced high streets would likely favour betweenness. The choice of correlate is another: land-use accessibility aligns conceptually with the proximity logic that closeness encodes, so variables such as pedestrian volumes or retail footfall may shift the relative performance of the formulations.

\subsection{Conclusion}

The divergence between cited closeness formulations and software implementations creates a reproducibility barrier in street network analysis. Researchers can reduce ambiguity by specifying which formulation is used and documenting computational methods precisely. Open code and data are provided to support replication and future benchmarking.
