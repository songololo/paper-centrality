\section{Discussion and Conclusions}\label{discussion}

The empirical results for Madrid are consistent with the theoretical hypothesis. Regarding \textbf{RQ1}, we find a meaningful distinction: mathematically defined \emph{Closeness} behaves opposite to its intended usage when applied to localised street network analysis, and when normalised in the form of \emph{Normalised Closeness}, it performs poorly compared to localised variants such as \emph{Improved Closeness} and \emph{Harmonic Closeness}. Regarding \textbf{RQ2}, the distinction is reflected in empirical associations: the results hold for land-use accessibility as well as for origin-destination trip counts, regardless of whether closeness centralities are length weighted or not, and irrespective of whether \emph{metric} or \emph{geometric} distances are used.

The NAIN formulation ($N^{1.2}$), introduced in \cite{Hillier2012}, represents an intermediate case. It performs better than \emph{Normalised Closeness} but does not fully recover the behaviour of \emph{Improved Closeness}. NAIN was derived empirically with the aim of aligning values across different distance radii, not to address the underlying scaling issue with varying subgraph sizes.

Developers of domain-specific urban network analysis packages appear to be aware of this issue even if not reflected in wider discussion; the \texttt{depthmapX} \cite{turner_depthmapx_2020}, \texttt{Place Syntax Tool} \cite{stahle_place_2023}, and \texttt{cityseer} \cite{simons_cityseer_2023} packages all use or offer a form of \emph{Improved Closeness} \cite{Wasserman1994}, and in some cases offer other suitable variants such as \emph{Harmonic Closeness} and the \emph{Gravity Index}.\footnote{Some packages also provide \emph{Normalised Closeness} as an option.} Researchers using these tools obtain correct results even when citing different formulations. The reproducibility problem instead affects researchers who implement closeness centrality from cited formulas using generic network packages, who attempt to replicate published studies without access to original code, or who review or teach network centrality methods based on commonly cited formulations.

We suspect that one of the reasons this misattribution persists is that even though \emph{Normalised Closeness} is typically cited by the authors of papers, these studies are often relying on computational packages which use forms of closeness that work for localised analysis. Consequently, outputs would typically perform as expected even if users were under mistaken impressions regarding the form of closeness being used. In cases where computational workflows rely on generic network analysis packages or where code is not openly available, this raises issues of reproducibility: researchers trying to replicate existing studies may be implementing an ineffective form of closeness centrality, and it may not be clear whether the cited formulation matches that used for the calculation process.

These ambiguities warrant further investigation into the robustness of currently held assumptions regarding the relative performance of different centralities and forms of distance heuristic. Earlier studies that cite \emph{Normalised Closeness} without providing open code, that select small geographic areas, or that do not buffer boundaries to control edge effects \cite{Turner2007, Porta2006} may warrant re-examination---not because their results are necessarily wrong, but because the relationship between cited methods and computed results is unclear. We acknowledge that some studies using \emph{Normalised Closeness} may have operated under conditions where the distinction matters less---for example, global analysis on fixed graphs, or localised analysis at thresholds where subgraph sizes vary minimally.

We consequently encourage the use of openly available reference datasets and reproducible workflows, as we have made available in this study, so that different measures can be benchmarked openly and collaboratively against common points of reference. This would facilitate the evaluation of newly available methods against established variants while accounting for differences which might otherwise be attributable to variations in urban context or data representation. It would also help uncover situations such as the widespread misconceptions on closeness centralities discussed in this study.

This study uses a single city (Madrid); correlation magnitudes will differ across urban contexts depending on street network morphology, density gradients, and land-use distributions. However, our contribution concerns the existence and direction of formulation differences, not their precise magnitude. The theoretical mechanism is mathematical: \emph{Closeness} conflates subgraph size with average distance; \emph{Normalised Closeness} neutralises meaningful variation; \emph{Improved Closeness} and \emph{Harmonic Closeness} avoid this conflation through different mechanisms---the former by dividing by average distance, the latter by summing distance reciprocals. This mechanism applies to any network where subgraph sizes vary by origin, which is why developers of domain-specific packages adopted these formulations.

In conclusion, our findings support a clear practical recommendation: researchers working with localised street network analysis should use \emph{Improved Closeness}, \emph{Harmonic Closeness}, or similar variants rather than \emph{Normalised Closeness}, and should clearly document and share the methods and precise computational formulations used in their analyses.
