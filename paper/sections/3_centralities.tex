\section{Closeness Centrality Formulations}\label{closeness_centrality}

\subsection{Divergence between cited and implemented formulations}

Application of the concept of closeness to street network analysis emerged with the development of Space Syntax theory in the late 1970s and early 1980s \cite{Hillier1984}. In this context, closeness is referred to as \emph{Integration} and implies ``to-movement'' potential or ``closeness'' in the general sense. (Throughout this paper, we use lowercase ``closeness'' for the general concept and italicised \emph{Closeness} for the specific mathematical formulation.) In the topological graph representations used at the time, \emph{Integration} was calculated by counting the number of topological steps to surrounding nodes, taking the average, then ``relativising'' the result to the size of the network, and taking the reciprocal, thereby converting the measure from farness-like to closeness-like.

The increasing availability of street network datasets in the 2000s prompted a shift away from topological representations towards segment-based representations, which derive the network directly from road centre-lines. Street segments are represented as nodes and the angular or metric distances between them serve as edge weights \cite{Turner2000, Turner2007}. The method for calculating \emph{Integration} was adapted accordingly, replacing topological step counts with accumulated angular deviation or metric distance. When adapting this to localised analysis, Turner initially suggested \emph{Normalised Closeness} (Equation~\ref{eq:normalised_closeness}) \citeyearpar{Turner2007}, but later proposed dividing the node count by the average distance to nodes \cite{turner_getting_2008}, also expressed as taking the average distance (\emph{Normalised Farness}), re-normalising by node count, then taking the reciprocal. Hillier and Turner et al.\ later confirm that \texttt{depthmapX} calculates integration by \emph{``dividing mean angular depth by node count\ldots\ and taking the reciprocal''} \cite{Hillier2012}. This variant resembles a simplified form of \emph{Improved Closeness} (Equations~\ref{eq:improved_closeness},~\ref{eq:used_closeness}) proposed by Wasserman and Faust \citeyearpar{Wasserman1994}, which differs from \emph{Normalised Closeness} in that it divides the node count by the \textbf{average} as opposed to \textbf{total} distance to nodes. At least three street network analysis packages implement this form: \texttt{depthmapX} \cite{Hillier2012, turner_depthmapx_2020}, \texttt{Place Syntax Tool} \cite{stahle_place_2023}, and \texttt{cityseer} \cite{simons_cityseer_2023}; \texttt{NetworkX} \cite{hagberg_exploring_2008} also uses it for disconnected graphs.

However, concurrent with this shift towards road centreline representations, researchers have consistently cited \emph{Normalised Closeness} when referring to closeness, even when using packages that compute \emph{Improved Closeness}. Citations for non-normalised \emph{Closeness} (Equation~\ref{eq:closeness}) and \emph{Normalised Farness} (Equation~\ref{eq:normalised_farness}) also appear. Table~\ref{table:closeness_literature} presents representative examples.

\subsection{Hypothesis}

Given this divergence, we ask whether these formulations behave differently under localised analysis---and whether the difference is consequential rather than merely notational. We hypothesise that \emph{Closeness} and \emph{Normalised Closeness} yield weak or directionally inconsistent results under localised analysis, while \emph{Harmonic Closeness} and \emph{Improved Closeness} scale more coherently with node count and proximity. The following theoretical account develops this argument.

The mathematical \emph{Closeness} measure
\begin{equation}\label{eq:closeness}
  Closeness_{(i)} = \frac{1}{\sum_{j\neq{i}}d_{(i,j)}}
\end{equation}
is the reciprocal of \emph{farness}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:farness}
  Farness_{(i)} = \sum_{j\neq{i}}d_{(i,j)}\, ,
\end{equation}
where \emph{Farness} is the sum of distances $d$ from node $i$ to all reachable nodes $j$ \cite{Sabidussi1966}. \emph{Closeness} conveys proximity to surrounding nodes, often interpreted as a proxy for potential access. It can be normalised by the number of nodes in the graph $N$ divided by the sum of distances
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normalised_closeness}
  Normalised\ Closeness_{(i)} = \frac{N-1}{\sum_{j\neq{i}}d_{(i,j)}}\ ,
\end{equation}
which is the inverse of the arithmetic mean of \emph{Farness}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normalised_farness}
  Normalised\ Farness_{(i)} = \frac{\sum_{j\neq{i}}d_{(i,j)}}{N-1}\ .
\end{equation}
It is common to use $N-1$ to exclude the origin node $i$ (though this only has a notable impact on small graphs).

In localised analysis, reachable node counts vary by origin. While \emph{Closeness} and \emph{Normalised Closeness} are commonly used for comparing nodes within a fixed graph, they can be difficult to interpret across differently sized subgraphs because they aggregate distances in ways that conflate node quantity with proximity. In the broader network analysis field, \emph{Harmonic Closeness} centrality has been proposed as a formulation less sensitive to subgraph size \cite{Marchiori2000, Rochat2009}, with the division happening prior to the summation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:harmonic_closeness}
  Harmonic\ Closeness_{(i)} = \sum_{j\neq{i}}\frac{1}{d_{(i,j)}}\ .
\end{equation}

For the same reason, others have suggested \emph{Improved Closeness} centrality \cite{Wasserman1994}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:improved_closeness}
  Improved\ C_{(i)} = \frac{N_{i}/{g}}{\sum_{j\neq{i}}d_{(i,j)}/{N_{i}}}\, ,
\end{equation}
the ratio of the fraction of reachable nodes $N_{i}/{g}$ to their average distance. In street network analysis, $g$ (the global node count) is constant across subgraphs and can be dropped, giving
\begin{equation}\label{eq:used_closeness}
  Improved\ C_{(i)} = \frac{N_{i}}{\sum_{j\neq{i}}d_{(i,j)}/{N_{i}}} = \frac{N_{i}^2}{\sum_{j\neq{i}}d_{(i,j)}}\ ,
\end{equation}
which is the variant found in computational packages and proposed by Turner and Hillier. \texttt{NetworkX} retains the full Wasserman and Faust scaling by $g$ \cite{hagberg_exploring_2008}.

The key distinction is that \emph{Improved Closeness} divides the number of reachable nodes by the \textbf{average} distance instead of the \textbf{total} distance. It therefore scales with both node count and proximity even when subgraph sizes vary: it increases with a greater number of locally accessible nodes and decreases with greater average distance to those nodes.

Figure~\ref{fig:closeness_comparisons} illustrates the implications: Scenario B would typically be interpreted as having higher closeness than Scenario A because it is equivalently close to a greater number of nodes. Scenario C contains a more distant node and would therefore have lower closeness than B but still greater than A.

As shown in Table~\ref{table:closeness_comparisons}, mathematical \emph{Closeness} yields a direction reversal: it decreases from A to B and from A to C, despite B and C having greater access. \emph{Normalised Closeness} neutralises meaningful variation (A to B) while exhibiting non-monotonic behaviour (decreasing from A to C). \emph{Harmonic Closeness} and \emph{Improved Closeness} yield values that increase with both node count and proximity.

Of these formulations, \emph{Harmonic Closeness} offers greater sensitivity to individual distances because it computes distance reciprocals before summation, in contrast to \emph{Improved Closeness}, which first averages the distances; yet, for the same reason, the averaging implicit with \emph{Improved Closeness} may be advantageous when working with poorer quality or unsimplified representations of street networks where individual distance reciprocals may otherwise become very large if a network segment approaches zero length.
