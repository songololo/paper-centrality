\section{Results}\label{data-analysis}

\subsection{Key Finding: Sign Reversal Across Formulations}

Block-bootstrap confidence intervals (accounting for spatial autocorrelation; see Section~\ref{sec:spatial_autocorr} and Table~\ref{tab:bootstrap_ci}) reveal a clear divergence in how closeness formulations associate with land-use accessibility at localised scales. The same sign pattern is visible for stand-alone Food \& Beverage and Retail measures (Figure~\ref{fig:cent_lu_corrs}).
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Closeness} shows a substantial \emph{negative} correlation ($\rho \approx \pn{RhoCloseness500D500}$, \pct{95} CI: $\pn{CICloseness500D500}$ at \distm{500}), a direction reversal relative to the usual interpretation.
  \item \textbf{Normalised Closeness} ($N^1$) shows correlations near zero ($\rho \approx \pn{RhoCloseN1500D500}$, \pct{95} CI: $\pn{CICloseN1500D500}$ at \distm{500}), suggesting that normalisation does not address the underlying issue.
  \item \textbf{Improved Closeness} ($N^2$), \textbf{Gravity}, and \textbf{Harmonic Closeness} show consistently \emph{positive} associations ($\rho \approx \pn{RhoCloseN2500D500}$, \pct{95} CI: $\pn{CICloseN2500D500}$ at \distm{500} for \emph{Improved Closeness}).
\end{itemize}
These confidence intervals account for the high spatial autocorrelation typical of closeness-like fields. At \distm{500}, Moran's $I$ ranges from approximately $\pn{MoranICloseness500D500}$ for \emph{Closeness} to $\pn{MoranICloseN2500D500}$ for \emph{Improved Closeness}, reducing the effective sample size from $N = \nSegments$ segments to approximately $\pn{NeffCloseN2500D500}$--$\pn{NeffCloseness500D500}$ independent observations depending on the formulation ($N_{\text{eff}} \approx N(1-I)/(1+I)$; see Table~\ref{tab:neff}). Spatial dependence is itself informative: formulations that align with the theoretical prediction (\emph{Improved}, \emph{Gravity}, \emph{Harmonic}) exhibit high spatial coherence, consistent with the smooth clustering expected of a closeness-like accessibility field, whereas \emph{Closeness} and \emph{Normalised Closeness} produce less coherent spatial patterning. Despite this reduced effective $N$, the block-bootstrap confidence intervals for \emph{Closeness}, \emph{Normalised Closeness}, and \emph{Improved Closeness} do not overlap, indicating a clear separation between these associations. This pattern persists across distance thresholds (\distm{500}--\distkm{10}), for both metric and geometric distances, and for origin-destination trip data.

\subsection{Visual Comparisons}

Figure~\ref{fig:closeness_compare} illustrates the \distm{1000} metric distance centralities. \emph{Closeness} exhibits direction reversal due to varying subgraph sizes. \emph{Normalised Closeness} and \emph{NAIN} flatten the distribution, limiting contrast. \emph{Improved}, \emph{Gravity}, and \emph{Harmonic} formulations assign higher values where more nodes are reachable at shorter distances. Angular variants exhibit the same pattern (Supplementary Materials, Figure~\ref{fig:closeness_compare_ang_supp}). The correlation matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:cent_corr_matrix_close}) reflects these differences, with \emph{Normalised Closeness} diverging markedly from other forms and \emph{Closeness} showing negative correlations at smaller distances.

\subsection{Correlations with Land-Use Accessibility}

Figure~\ref{fig:cent_lu_corrs} presents correlation grids comparing metric distance centralities to land-use accessibilities (angular and length-weighted variants in Supplementary Materials, Figures~\ref{fig:cent_lu_corrs_ang}--\ref{fig:cent_lu_corrs_length_wtd_ang}). Each cell shows a Spearman correlation between a centrality measure ($y$ axis) at a given distance threshold ($x$ axis) and the indicated land-use theme.

\begin{figure*}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth, keepaspectratio]{plots/cent_lu_corrs.pdf}
  \caption{Correlation grids comparing \emph{metric distance} network centralities to land uses.}\label{fig:cent_lu_corrs}
\end{figure*}

Key patterns:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Farness} is positively associated, implying its inverse \emph{Closeness} is negatively associated. The normalised case yields negligible associations.
  \item \emph{NAIN} ($N^{1.2}$) shows stronger associations than \emph{Normalised Closeness} but generally below \emph{Improved Closeness}, \emph{Gravity}, and \emph{Harmonic Closeness}, which show the most consistently positive associations. Differences between the latter three are small, and we do not treat them as grounds to prefer one over another.
  \item Length-weighted variants exhibit similar behaviour with generally slightly stronger associations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Correlations with Origin-Destination Trip Data}

Correlations with travel survey data (Figure~\ref{fig:cent_ts_corrs}; angular variants in Figure~\ref{fig:cent_ts_corrs_ang}) reflect the same pattern: \emph{Closeness} and \emph{Normalised Closeness} show negative or negligible associations, while \emph{Improved}, \emph{Gravity}, and \emph{Harmonic} formulations show larger-magnitude positive associations.
