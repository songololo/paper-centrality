\subsection{Additional Centralities}\label{additional-centralities}

\subsubsection{The \emph{Gravity Index}}

The \emph{Gravity Index} measures accessibility using spatial impedance \cite{Batty2013, Hansen1959, Rutherford1979}. Structurally similar to \emph{Harmonic Closeness}---both sum distance-weighted contributions from reachable nodes and both account for varying subgraph sizes under localised analysis---the \emph{Gravity Index} offers explicit control over distance decay via the negative exponential (Equation~\ref{eq:gravity}), where the decay rate $\beta$ can be calibrated to specific trip purposes or transportation modes \cite{Sevtsuk2012, Handy1997, Iacono2008}. Various impedance functions behave similarly \cite{vale_influence_2017}.

Despite the name, the \emph{Gravity Index} assumes equal attraction from each node, unlike full gravity models that account for origin--destination attraction, and should not be confused with them. It provides distance-weighted counts of accessible locations $j$ from $i$ at impedance $\beta$, and varying the decay parameter emphasises smaller or larger network structures.

  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth, keepaspectratio]{images/gravity_decay.pdf}
  \caption{Spatial impedance curves for different $\beta$ parameters. Nearer locations can be weighted more heavily than farther locations through use of the negative exponential decay function.}\label{fig:beta_decays}

Gravity measures are inherently localised and do not strictly require distance cutoffs, though computational efficiency favours thresholds where decay renders contributions negligible (Figure~\ref{fig:beta_decays}). Here, $\beta = 4 / d_{max}$, giving a characteristic distance of $d_{max}/4$ and a residual weight at $d_{max}$ of $\exp(-4)\approx0.018$. Calibrating for pedestrians, $\beta=0.005$ ($d_{\max}=800m$) corresponds to commonly cited walking distances to bus stops \cite{Sevtsuk2016, Handy1997}. Willingness to walk varies by trip purpose and location \cite{Iacono2008, Reyer2014}, but pedestrians are generally unwilling to walk more than ${\sim}1600m$ ($\beta\approx0.0025$) for non-recreational purposes \cite{Baradaran2001, Scheurer2007, Duncan2011, Handy2001, Harris2001, Bates2007}.

\subsubsection{Betweenness centrality}

\emph{Betweenness} \cite{Freeman1977} counts the fraction of shortest paths between all $(j, k)$ pairs that pass through $i$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:betweenness}
  Betweenness_{(i)} = \sum_{j\neq{i}} \sum_{k\neq{j}\neq{i}} \frac{\sigma_{jk}(i)}{\sigma_{jk}}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{jk}$ is the number of shortest paths between $j$ and $k$ and $\sigma_{jk}(i)$ the number passing through $i$. The Space Syntax measure \emph{Choice} is similar in intent but differs in its computation \cite{stahle_place_2023}. Localised \emph{Betweenness} considers only $(j, k)$ pairs within threshold distances.

\emph{Betweenness} can be distance-weighted using the negative exponential (Equation~\ref{eq:gravity}), reflecting that trips between nearby $(j, k)$ pairs are more likely:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weighted_betweenness}
  Betweenness_{(i)}=\sum_{j\neq{i}}\sum_{k\neq{j}\neq{i}} \frac{\sigma_{jk}(i)}{\sigma_{jk}}\cdot\exp(-\beta\cdot d_{(j,k)})\, ,
\end{equation}
where $d_{(j,k)}$ is the shortest-path distance between $j$ and $k$.

Space Syntax also includes \emph{NACH} (normalised angular choice), dividing \emph{Betweenness} (Choice) by \emph{Farness} (Total Depth):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:nach}
  NACH{(i)}=\frac{\log(Betweenness + 1)}{\log(Farness + 3)}.
\end{equation}
This is more accurately a hybrid betweenness--closeness measure than a normalisation: \emph{``it seems to combine our two measures -- depth and choice, to and through-movement''} \cite{Hillier2012}. The constants guard against logarithms of values less than 1.

\subsubsection{Length-weighting}
Varying node intensities can distort centrality measures. Weighting by street lengths \cite{Turner2005a, Turner2007} or building counts \cite{Sevtsuk2012} corrects for this. In the dual representation, primal segment lengths are assigned to dual nodes. For \emph{Harmonic Closeness}, the $1$ in the numerator is replaced by segment length $l$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:harmonic_length_weighted}
  HC_{(i)} = \sum_{j\neq{i}}\frac{l}{d_{(i,j)}}\, .
\end{equation}

An alternative uses the integral of \emph{Harmonic Closeness}, summing the area under the impedance curve for segment bounds $a$ and $b$ across all reachable segments $S$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:harmonic_integral}
  HC_{(i)} = \sum_{(a, b)\in S} \int_{a}^{b}\ \frac{1}{x}\ dx = \sum_{(a, b)\in S}\ \ln(b) - \ln(a)\, .
\end{equation}
This continuous form is consistent regardless of how segments are subdivided (Figure~\ref{fig:closeness_comparisons_length_weighted}, Table~\ref{table:closeness_comparisons_length_weighted}). For example, a segment from $1m$ to $10m$ contributes $\ln(10)=2.303$; one from $10m$ to $20m$ contributes $0.693$. Segment bounds are strictly positive under dual representations with segment midpoints, or can use a small $\epsilon$-offset to avoid $\ln(0)$.

The continuous form of \emph{Harmonic Closeness} (Equation~\ref{eq:harmonic_integral}) cannot be used with \emph{geometric distance} (angular) impedances, which do not increase continuously.

The gravity index can also be expressed in continuous form for segments $S$ with bounds $a$ and $b$ at impedance $\beta$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gravity_continuous}
  G_{(i)} = \sum_{(a, b)\in S} \int_{a}^{b}\ f(x)\ dx = \sum_{(a, b)\in S}\ \frac{\exp(-\beta\cdot b) -\exp(-\beta\cdot a)}{-\beta}.
\end{equation}
